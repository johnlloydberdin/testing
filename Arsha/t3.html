Balangay Shrine Museum

"Evidence of maritime trade, boat-building skills and seamanship long before Spanish colonial times"

The Balanghai Shrine displays a Butuan boat (balangay or balanghai) carbon-dated to 320 A.D. This style of boat-building has been found from Scandinavia through the South Pacific in the 3rd century, a technology still used by present- day boatmakers of Sibutu Island and in southern Philippines. The Butuan boats were made of hardwood, can seat 25 people and were built to withstand long voyages.

They provide evidence of maritime trade, boat-building skills and seamanship long before Spanish colonial times. To date, 9 balanghai have been discovered, of which 3 have been excavated and conserved by the National Museum. Carbon-dating showed the other 2 boats' ages at 990 A.D. and 1250 A.D.

Fast-forward to 2009. A nonprofit organization (Kaya ng Pinoy Foundation) created replicas of the balanghai and sailed around the Philippine archipelago and neighboring countries. Balangay Shrine Museum is a one-story building that serves as a unit of the National Museum. It is located in Barangay Libertad and it houses the remnants of Seagoing Outrigger Boat or Balangay. Small but informative museum with few artifacts from the area. It would better serve the public if the chronology was better laid out for presentation. Over all, it was informative.

The balangay was the first wooden boat that was excavated in Southeast Asia. These boats were instrumental in the settlement of Austronesian peoples in the Philippines and the Malay archipelago. It was used for cargo and trading, in which Butuan, Agusan de Norte, Philippines was a central trading port.

Previously, balangay boats were discovered in the late 1970s in Butuan City by archaeologists from the National Museum.
